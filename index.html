Syllabus Overview

1.1 Business activity

Business activity involves the production and exchange of goods and services to satisfy human needs and wants.

  • Needs: Essential items for survival (e.g., food, water, shelter)
  • Wants: Desires that enhance quality of life but aren't essential for survival
  • Scarcity: The basic economic problem that resources are limited but wants are unlimited
  • Opportunity cost: The next best alternative given up when making a choice

Importance of specialization: Specialization in business means focusing on a particular product, service, or production process. It leads to increased efficiency and productivity.

Purpose of business activity: To produce goods or provide services that satisfy customer needs and wants while making a profit.

The concept of adding value: Adding value is the process of increasing the worth of a product or service. This can be done through manufacturing, marketing, or providing excellent customer service.

How does specialization contribute to business efficiency?

Specialization contributes to business efficiency by allowing workers and businesses to focus on specific tasks or products. This focus leads to increased expertise, faster production times, and often higher quality output. It also allows for the development of specialized tools and processes, further enhancing efficiency.

1.2 Classification of businesses

Businesses can be classified into different economic sectors:

  • Primary sector: Extraction of raw materials (e.g., mining, farming)
  • Secondary sector: Manufacturing and construction
  • Tertiary sector: Provision of services

In a mixed economy, businesses can be classified as:

  • Private sector: Owned and operated by individuals or groups
  • Public sector: Owned and operated by the government

Why is the tertiary sector growing in many developed economies?

The tertiary sector is growing in many developed economies due to several factors: increased automation in primary and secondary sectors, rising consumer demand for services, the growth of knowledge-based industries, and the outsourcing of manufacturing to developing countries. This shift reflects changing economic structures and consumer preferences in developed nations.

1.3 Enterprise, business growth and size

Enterprise: The willingness to take risks and use initiative to start a business.

Characteristics of successful entrepreneurs include:

  • Risk-taking
  • Innovation
  • Decision-making skills
  • Organizational skills

Business plan: A document that outlines the business idea, market analysis, financial projections, and operational details.

Government support for business start-ups: Governments support new businesses through grants, training schemes, and tax incentives to promote economic growth and job creation.

Measuring business size: Business size can be measured by:

  • Number of employees
  • Value of sales/turnover
  • Value of capital employed

Business growth: Businesses may grow through internal expansion (organic growth) or external growth (mergers and acquisitions).

What are the potential challenges of rapid business growth?

Rapid business growth can present several challenges: cash flow problems due to increased costs, difficulties in maintaining quality control, strain on management and organizational structures, potential loss of company culture, increased competition, and the need for new skills and resources. Managing these challenges is crucial for sustainable growth.

1.4 Types of business organisation

Different forms of business organization include:

  • Sole traders: Owned and run by one person
  • Partnerships: Owned and run by 2-20 people (unlimited liability)
  • Private limited companies (Ltd): Privately owned, shares not sold to general public
  • Public limited companies (Plc): Can sell shares to the general public
  • Franchises: Business format sold by a franchisor to a franchisee

Limited liability: Means that the owners' personal assets are protected if the business fails.

What are the main advantages and disadvantages of a franchise business model?

Advantages of franchising:

  • Established brand and business model
  • Support from franchisor (training, marketing)
  • Lower risk compared to starting a new business
Disadvantages of franchising:
  • High initial costs and ongoing fees
  • Limited control and creativity
  • Dependence on franchisor's reputation

1.5 Business objectives and stakeholder objectives

Business objectives may include:

  • Survival
  • Profit maximization
  • Growth
  • Market share
  • Customer satisfaction

Stakeholders: Individuals or groups with an interest in the business. They include:

  • Shareholders: Want high dividends and increasing share value
  • Employees: Want job security, good pay, and working conditions
  • Customers: Want good quality products at reasonable prices
  • Suppliers: Want regular orders and prompt payment
  • Government: Wants businesses to follow regulations and pay taxes
  • Local community: Wants job creation and minimal environmental impact

Objectives of private sector enterprises often focus on profit, while public sector objectives may prioritize service provision and social welfare.

How might the objectives of different stakeholders conflict with each other?

Stakeholder objectives can conflict in various ways. For example:

  • Shareholders wanting higher profits might conflict with employees wanting higher wages
  • Customers desiring lower prices might conflict with suppliers wanting higher payments
  • The local community's environmental concerns might conflict with the business's growth objectives
  • Government's regulatory requirements might conflict with the business's desire to minimize costs
Balancing these conflicting objectives is a key challenge for business management.

2.1 Motivating employees

The importance of a well-motivated workforce: A motivated workforce is crucial for business success as it leads to increased productivity, reduced absenteeism, and lower staff turnover.

Human needs and motivation: Understanding human needs is essential for effective motivation. Maslow's hierarchy of needs is a key theory in this area:

  • Physiological needs (food, water, shelter)
  • Safety needs (security, stability)
  • Love and belonging needs (friendship, family)
  • Esteem needs (respect, recognition)
  • Self-actualization needs (personal growth, fulfillment)

Motivational theories:

  • Taylor's theory: Focuses on financial rewards as the primary motivator
  • Herzberg's two-factor theory: Distinguishes between hygiene factors (which prevent dissatisfaction) and motivators (which create satisfaction)

Methods of motivation:

  • Financial rewards: wage, salary, bonus, commission, profit sharing
  • Non-financial methods: job enrichment, job rotation, teamworking, training, promotion opportunities

How might Herzberg's two-factor theory be applied in a workplace?

Applying Herzberg's two-factor theory in a workplace involves addressing both hygiene factors and motivators:
Hygiene factors: Ensure fair wages, good working conditions, job security, and effective company policies to prevent dissatisfaction.
Motivators: Provide opportunities for achievement, recognition, responsibility, advancement, and personal growth to create satisfaction and motivation. By addressing both aspects, employers can create a work environment that not only prevents dissatisfaction but also actively motivates employees.

2.2 Organisation and management

Organisational structure: The way a business is organized, including levels of hierarchy, span of control, and chain of command.

Organisational charts: Visual representations of the structure of an organization, showing relationships between different positions and departments.

Roles and responsibilities:

  • Directors: Responsible for overall strategy and major decisions
  • Managers: Implement strategies and oversee day-to-day operations
  • Supervisors: Direct and oversee the work of other employees
  • Operational employees: Carry out the main work of the organization

The role of management: Key functions include planning, organizing, coordinating, commanding, and controlling.

Leadership styles:

  • Autocratic: Leader makes all decisions without consulting others
  • Democratic: Leader involves team members in decision-making
  • Laissez-faire: Leader delegates decision-making to team members

What are the potential advantages and disadvantages of a democratic leadership style?

Advantages of democratic leadership:

  • Increased employee engagement and job satisfaction
  • Better decision-making through diverse input
  • Encourages creativity and innovation
  • Develops leadership skills in employees
Disadvantages of democratic leadership:
  • Decision-making can be slower
  • May lead to confusion if there's no clear final decision-maker
  • Not suitable for situations requiring quick decisions
  • Can be less effective with inexperienced team members

2.3 Recruitment, selection and training of employees

Recruitment: The process of finding and attracting suitable candidates for a job.

Selection: The process of choosing the most suitable candidate for a job.

Recruitment methods:

  • Internal recruitment (promoting from within)
  • External recruitment (hiring from outside the organization)
  • Methods include job advertisements, recruitment agencies, job fairs, and online platforms

Selection process:

  • Reviewing applications and CVs
  • Shortlisting candidates
  • Interviews
  • Tests and assessments
  • Reference checks

Training: The process of improving an employee's skills, knowledge, and capabilities.

Types of training:

  • Induction training: For new employees
  • On-the-job training: Learning while working
  • Off-the-job training: Learning away from the normal work environment

What are the potential benefits and drawbacks of internal recruitment?

Benefits of internal recruitment:

  • Lower recruitment costs
  • Shorter adjustment period as employee knows the company
  • Boosts employee morale and motivation
  • Retains organizational knowledge
Drawbacks of internal recruitment:
  • Limited pool of candidates
  • May lead to internal politics or resentment
  • Lack of fresh perspectives and ideas from outside
  • Creates another vacancy that needs to be filled

2.4 Internal and external communication

Internal communication: The exchange of information within an organization.

External communication: The exchange of information between an organization and outside parties.

Methods of communication:

  • Verbal: Face-to-face conversations, telephone calls, video conferences
  • Written: Letters, emails, reports, memos
  • Visual: Presentations, charts, graphs
  • Non-verbal: Body language, facial expressions

Barriers to effective communication:

  • Language or cultural differences
  • Technical jargon
  • Poor listening skills
  • Information overload
  • Physical barriers (e.g., noise)

Importance of effective communication: Crucial for coordinating activities, motivating employees, building relationships with customers and suppliers, and overall business success.

How might a business overcome barriers to effective communication?

A business can overcome communication barriers through several strategies:

  • Use clear and simple language, avoiding unnecessary jargon
  • Encourage active listening and provide training in communication skills
  • Use multiple channels of communication to reinforce messages
  • Create an open culture where feedback is encouraged
  • Use visual aids to support complex information
  • Be aware of and respect cultural differences
  • Regularly check understanding and clarify when necessary
  • Improve the physical environment to reduce noise and distractions

Flashcard: Motivation Theories

Compare Taylor's theory and Herzberg's two-factor theory of motivation.

Taylor's Theory:

  • Focuses on financial rewards
  • Assumes workers are primarily motivated by money
  • Emphasizes efficiency and productivity

Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory:

  • Distinguishes between hygiene factors and motivators
  • Hygiene factors prevent dissatisfaction (e.g., salary, working conditions)
  • Motivators create satisfaction (e.g., recognition, achievement)
  • Suggests both factors need to be addressed for true motivation

3.1 Marketing, competition and the customer

The role of marketing: Marketing is the process of identifying, anticipating and satisfying customer needs profitably. It involves:

  • Identifying customer needs
  • Developing products or services to meet these needs
  • Promoting products or services to potential customers
  • Ensuring customer satisfaction to build loyalty

Market changes: Markets are dynamic and can change due to various factors:

  • Changes in consumer tastes and preferences
  • Technological advancements
  • Economic factors (e.g., income levels, inflation)
  • Demographic changes
  • Competitive actions

Concepts of niche marketing and mass marketing:

  • Niche marketing: Targeting a specific, small segment of a market
  • Mass marketing: Targeting the entire market with one product or marketing strategy

Market segmentation: The process of dividing a market into distinct groups of consumers with different needs, characteristics, or behaviors. Segmentation can be based on:

  • Demographics (age, gender, income)
  • Psychographics (lifestyle, values, attitudes)
  • Geographic location
  • Behavioral factors (buying patterns, brand loyalty)

What are the potential advantages and disadvantages of niche marketing?

Advantages of niche marketing:

  • Less competition in specialized markets
  • Higher customer loyalty due to specialized products
  • Potential for higher profit margins
  • More efficient use of marketing resources
Disadvantages of niche marketing:
  • Limited market size and growth potential
  • Higher risk if the niche market declines
  • Potentially higher production costs due to smaller scale
  • Risk of larger competitors entering the niche

3.2 Market research

The role of market research: Market research is the process of gathering, analyzing and interpreting information about a market, product or service to be offered for sale in that market. It includes research into customer characteristics, buying habits, and preferences.

Methods of market research:

  • Primary research: First-hand data collection
    • Surveys and questionnaires
    • Interviews
    • Focus groups
    • Observations
  • Secondary research: Using existing data
    • Government publications
    • Trade journals
    • Competitor websites
    • Market reports

Sampling: The process of selecting a representative group from the population to conduct research.

Presentation and use of market research results: Data can be presented in various formats such as graphs, charts, and reports. Results are used to inform marketing decisions, product development, and business strategy.

Why might a business choose to use secondary research instead of primary research?

A business might choose secondary research over primary research for several reasons:

  • Cost-effective: Generally cheaper than conducting primary research
  • Time-saving: Data is already collected and often analyzed
  • Provides background information: Useful for understanding market trends and competitor activities
  • Access to larger datasets: Can provide a broader perspective than a single company's primary research
  • Useful for initial exploration: Can help refine questions for subsequent primary research
However, secondary research may not always provide the specific information needed, and the data might not be as current or relevant to the specific business context.

3.3 Marketing mix

The marketing mix: Also known as the 4Ps, it consists of:

  • Product: What the business offers to customers
    • Features and benefits
    • Branding
    • Packaging
    • Product life cycle
  • Price: How much customers pay
    • Pricing strategies (e.g., skimming, penetration, competition-based)
    • Discounts and offers
    • Price elasticity of demand
  • Place: How and where the product is sold
    • Distribution channels
    • Retail locations
    • E-commerce
  • Promotion: How the product is advertised and sold
    • Advertising
    • Sales promotion
    • Public relations
    • Personal selling

Product life cycle: The stages a product goes through in the market:

  • Development
  • Introduction
  • Growth
  • Maturity
  • Decline

Extension strategies: Methods to extend the product life cycle, such as finding new uses, markets, or making product modifications.

How might a company's marketing mix change as a product moves from the introduction stage to the growth stage of its life cycle?

As a product moves from introduction to growth stage:

  • Product: May introduce product variations or improvements based on early customer feedback
  • Price: Might adjust pricing strategy, possibly lowering prices to attract more customers as competition increases
  • Place: Likely to expand distribution channels to reach a wider market
  • Promotion: Shift from awareness-building to emphasizing product benefits and brand differentiation
The focus generally shifts from educating consumers about the new product to persuading them to choose it over competing options.

3.4 Marketing strategy

Elements of marketing strategy:

  • Target market selection
  • Market positioning
  • Marketing mix decisions
  • Marketing objectives

Influence of marketing on consumer decisions: Marketing can impact consumer awareness, preferences, and purchasing behavior.

Legal controls related to marketing: Regulations that affect marketing practices, such as truth in advertising laws, data protection regulations, and industry-specific rules.

Entering new markets: Strategies for expanding into new geographical areas or customer segments, including considerations of cultural differences and local preferences.

What factors should a business consider when deciding to enter a new foreign market?

When entering a new foreign market, a business should consider:

  • Political and economic stability of the country
  • Legal and regulatory environment
  • Cultural differences and local customs
  • Market size and growth potential
  • Competition in the local market
  • Infrastructure and logistics
  • Potential entry barriers (e.g., tariffs, local partnerships)
  • Currency exchange rates and economic factors
  • Availability of skilled workforce
  • Adaptation needs for products or services
These factors help assess the potential opportunities and risks associated with entering the new market.

4.1 Production of goods and services

The meaning of production: The process of creating goods or providing services.

Methods of production:

  • Job production: Making one-off or customized products
  • Batch production: Making groups of identical products
  • Flow production: Continuous production of standardized products

Productivity: A measure of the efficiency of production, typically calculated as output per unit of input.

Efficiency: Producing goods or services with minimum waste of time and resources.

Lean production: A systematic method for waste minimization within a manufacturing system without sacrificing productivity.

Just-in-time (JIT) inventory management: A strategy to increase efficiency and decrease waste by receiving goods only as they are needed in the production process.

What are the potential benefits and drawbacks of implementing a just-in-time inventory system?

Benefits of JIT:

  • Reduced inventory costs
  • Less warehouse space needed
  • Improved cash flow
  • Reduced waste from obsolete inventory
  • Faster detection of defects
Drawbacks of JIT:
  • Risk of production halts if supplies are delayed
  • Less flexibility to meet sudden increases in demand
  • Dependence on reliable suppliers
  • Potential for higher transportation costs
  • Requires significant planning and coordination

4.2 Costs, scale of production and break-even analysis

Types of costs:

  • Fixed costs: Costs that do not vary with output
  • Variable costs: Costs that change with the level of output
  • Total costs: Fixed costs + Variable costs
  • Average costs: Total costs divided by output

Economies of scale: The cost advantages that businesses obtain due to their scale of operation, with cost per unit of output decreasing with increasing scale.

Diseconomies of scale: The increase in average costs as a business grows beyond its optimum size.

Break-even analysis: A technique for determining the point at which revenue equals total costs (fixed + variable).

Break-even chart: A graphical representation showing the level of output at which total costs equal total revenue.

How can break-even analysis be useful for business decision-making?

Break-even analysis can be useful for business decision-making in several ways:

  • Helps determine the minimum level of sales needed to cover costs
  • Assists in setting prices for products or services
  • Guides decisions about production levels
  • Helps assess the impact of changes in costs or prices
  • Aids in evaluating new product ideas or investment opportunities
  • Provides a simple way to analyze and communicate financial information
However, it's important to note that break-even analysis has limitations, such as assuming a constant selling price and linear cost functions, which may not always reflect real-world complexities.

4.3 Achieving quality production

Quality: The standard of a product or service, and its fitness for purpose.

Quality control: Checking output against required standards.

Quality assurance: Preventing errors by creating a system in which they are unlikely to arise.

Total Quality Management (TQM): A comprehensive approach to long-term success through customer satisfaction, involving all members of an organization in improving processes, products, services, and the culture in which they work.

Benchmarking: Comparing one's business processes and performance metrics to industry bests and best practices from other companies.

How might implementing a Total Quality Management system benefit a manufacturing company?

Implementing TQM can benefit a manufacturing company in several ways:

  • Improved product quality and consistency
  • Increased customer satisfaction and loyalty
  • Reduced waste and lower costs
  • Enhanced employee engagement and job satisfaction
  • Improved processes and efficiency
  • Better problem-solving and decision-making across the organization
  • Stronger competitive position in the market
  • Continuous improvement culture
However, implementing TQM requires significant commitment, time, and resources, and may face resistance to change from some employees.

4.4 Location decisions

Factors influencing business location:

  • Proximity to raw materials
  • Availability of labor
  • Transport infrastructure
  • Proximity to markets
  • Government incentives
  • Cost of land or rent
  • Legal and regulatory environment
  • Environmental factors

Factors influencing relocation: Similar to location factors, but also including:

  • Changes in market demand
  • Technological advancements
  • Cost-saving opportunities
  • Expansion needs

Why might a manufacturing business choose to relocate its production to a different country?

A manufacturing business might choose to relocate its production to a different country for several reasons:

  • Lower labor costs in the new location
  • Access to cheaper raw materials
  • More favorable tax regimes or government incentives
  • Proximity to growing markets
  • Less stringent regulations (though this can be controversial)
  • Improved logistics or transportation infrastructure
  • Access to specialized skills or technology
  • To diversify political or economic risks
However, relocation also carries risks such as cultural differences, quality control issues, increased transportation costs, and potential negative publicity in the home country.

5.1 Business finance: needs and sources

The need for business finance: Businesses need finance for various reasons, including:

  • Start-up costs
  • Expansion
  • Cash flow management
  • Purchase of assets
  • Research and development

Sources of finance: Can be categorized as internal or external, and short-term or long-term.

Internal sources:

  • Retained profits
  • Sale of assets
  • Owner's investment

External sources:

  • Bank loans and overdrafts
  • Trade credit
  • Share issue
  • Venture capital
  • Leasing
  • Government grants
  • Crowdfunding

Short-term finance: Funding needed for less than one year (e.g., overdrafts, trade credit)

Long-term finance: Funding needed for more than one year (e.g., loans, share issues)

What factors should a business consider when choosing between different sources of finance?

When choosing between different sources of finance, a business should consider:

  • Cost of finance (interest rates, fees)
  • Amount of money needed
  • Length of time the finance is required
  • Purpose of the finance
  • Current financial position of the business
  • Risk associated with each source
  • Control implications (e.g., giving up equity in share issues)
  • Flexibility and repayment terms
  • Speed of accessing the finance
  • Legal and regulatory requirements
The best choice often depends on the specific circumstances and needs of the business.

5.2 Cash-flow forecasting and working capital

Cash flow: The movement of money in and out of a business.

Cash-flow forecast: A prediction of future cash flows, usually on a monthly basis.

Importance of cash-flow forecasting:

  • Anticipating cash shortages or surpluses
  • Planning for capital expenditure
  • Managing borrowing
  • Making informed business decisions

Working capital: The capital available for conducting the day-to-day operations of a business, calculated as current assets minus current liabilities.

Importance of working capital:

  • Ensures smooth day-to-day operations
  • Helps meet short-term obligations
  • Indicates the liquidity of a business

How might a business deal with a forecasted cash-flow shortage?

A business could deal with a forecasted cash-flow shortage in several ways:

  • Arranging a short-term bank overdraft
  • Delaying non-essential purchases
  • Negotiating extended credit terms with suppliers
  • Offering discounts for early payment to debtors
  • Selling excess inventory
  • Factoring receivables
  • Seeking additional investment or loans
  • Reducing costs where possible
  • Improving credit control procedures
The best approach depends on the specific circumstances and the severity of the shortage.

5.3 Income statements

Income statement (Profit and Loss Account): A financial statement that shows a business's revenues, costs, and expenses over a specific period.

Key components:

  • Revenue (Sales)
  • Cost of sales
  • Gross profit
  • Operating expenses
  • Operating profit
  • Interest and tax
  • Net profit

Importance of profit:

  • Measure of business success
  • Source of finance for growth
  • Attracts investors
  • Ensures long-term survival

How might a business aim to improve its net profit?

A business can aim to improve its net profit through various strategies:

  • Increasing sales revenue (e.g., through marketing, new products, or price increases)
  • Reducing cost of sales (e.g., negotiating better deals with suppliers, improving efficiency)
  • Cutting operating expenses (e.g., reducing overhead costs, improving productivity)
  • Diversifying income streams
  • Improving inventory management
  • Implementing cost-effective technologies
  • Refinancing to reduce interest expenses
  • Optimizing tax strategies (within legal limits)
It's important to balance profit improvement efforts with maintaining quality and customer satisfaction.

5.4 Statement of financial position

Statement of financial position (Balance Sheet): A financial statement that provides a snapshot of a business's assets, liabilities, and owner's equity at a specific point in time.

Key components:

  • Assets (fixed assets, current assets)
  • Liabilities (current liabilities, long-term liabilities)
  • Owner's equity (share capital, retained earnings)

Uses of the statement of financial position:

  • Assessing the financial health of a business
  • Calculating key financial ratios
  • Making investment decisions
  • Evaluating the capital structure

What information can be derived from a company's statement of financial position?

A company's statement of financial position can provide several key insights:

  • The overall financial position (net worth) of the business
  • The liquidity of the business (ability to meet short-term obligations)
  • The solvency of the business (ability to meet long-term obligations)
  • The capital structure (mix of debt and equity financing)
  • The value of assets owned by the business
  • The level of current and long-term liabilities
  • The amount of retained earnings (accumulated profits)
  • Information for calculating financial ratios (e.g., current ratio, debt-to-equity ratio)
This information is crucial for management, investors, and creditors in assessing the financial health and stability of the business.

5.5 Analysis of accounts

Financial ratios: Tools used to analyze financial statements and assess a company's performance and financial health.

Key ratios:

  • Profitability ratios:
    • Gross profit margin
    • Net profit margin
    • Return on Capital Employed (ROCE)
  • Liquidity ratios:
    • Current ratio
    • Acid test ratio
  • Efficiency ratios:
    • Inventory turnover
    • Debtor days
    • Creditor days

Uses of ratio analysis:

  • Comparing performance over time
  • Benchmarking against competitors
  • Identifying areas for improvement
  • Making informed business decisions

How might a low current ratio affect a business, and what steps could be taken to improve it?

A low current ratio (below 1:1) can affect a business in several ways:

  • Difficulty in meeting short-term obligations
  • Reduced creditworthiness
  • Potential cash flow problems
  • Difficulty in obtaining additional financing
Steps to improve the current ratio:
  • Increase current assets:
    • Improve inventory management
    • Collect accounts receivable more quickly
    • Sell unnecessary fixed assets
  • Reduce current liabilities:
    • Negotiate longer payment terms with suppliers
    • Refinance short-term debt into long-term debt
    • Improve overall cash management
It's important to maintain a balance, as an excessively high current ratio might indicate inefficient use of assets.

6.1 Economic issues

Business cycle: The fluctuations in economic activity that an economy experiences over time. The main stages are:

  • Boom
  • Recession
  • Slump
  • Recovery

Impact of economic changes:

  • Employment levels: Affects consumer spending and labor costs
  • Inflation: Impacts prices, wages, and purchasing power
  • Exchange rates: Affects international trade and competitiveness
  • Interest rates: Influences borrowing costs and investment decisions

Government economic objectives:

  • Economic growth
  • Low unemployment
  • Low inflation
  • Balance of payments stability

How might a period of high inflation affect businesses?

High inflation can affect businesses in several ways:

  • Increased costs of raw materials and supplies
  • Pressure to increase wages to maintain employee purchasing power
  • Difficulty in pricing products due to rapidly changing costs
  • Reduced consumer purchasing power, potentially leading to lower sales
  • Uncertainty in long-term planning and investment decisions
  • Potential for higher interest rates, increasing borrowing costs
  • Possible erosion of profit margins if unable to pass on cost increases to customers
  • Changes in consumer behavior, such as switching to cheaper alternatives
Businesses may need to adapt their strategies, such as increasing efficiency, hedging against price increases, or adjusting their product mix to cope with inflationary pressures.

6.2 Environmental and ethical issues

Environmental concerns:

  • Climate change and carbon emissions
  • Resource depletion
  • Pollution and waste management
  • Biodiversity loss

Ethical issues:

  • Fair trade
  • Labor practices and working conditions
  • Animal welfare
  • Corporate governance and transparency

Sustainable development: Meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.

Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR): A company's commitment to manage the social, environmental and economic effects of its operations responsibly and in line with public expectations.

How might a business benefit from implementing strong environmental and ethical policies?

Implementing strong environmental and ethical policies can benefit a business in several ways:

  • Enhanced reputation and brand image
  • Increased customer loyalty and potentially higher sales
  • Improved employee morale and attraction of talent
  • Cost savings through efficiency and waste reduction
  • Better risk management and compliance with regulations
  • Potential for innovation in products and processes
  • Improved relationships with stakeholders (investors, community, government)
  • Potential competitive advantage in the market
  • Access to new markets or customer segments that value sustainability
  • Long-term business sustainability
However, implementing these policies may require significant investment and changes in business practices, which need to be balanced against the potential benefits.

6.3 Business and the international economy

Globalisation: The increasing interconnectedness of the world's economies, cultures, and populations.

Impacts of globalisation on businesses:

  • Access to new markets
  • Increased competition
  • Opportunities for outsourcing and offshoring
  • Greater exposure to global economic fluctuations
  • Cultural challenges in international operations

Multinational companies (MNCs): Businesses that operate in multiple countries.

Reasons for the growth of MNCs:

  • Access to new markets
  • Economies of scale
  • Access to resources and labor
  • Risk diversification

Impact of MNCs on host countries:

  • Job creation
  • Technology transfer
  • Economic growth
  • Potential exploitation of resources or labor

Exchange rates: The value of one currency in relation to another.

Impact of exchange rate changes:

  • Affects the competitiveness of exports
  • Influences the cost of imports
  • Impacts on tourism and international travel
  • Affects the value of overseas investments

How might a significant appreciation of a country's currency affect businesses operating in that country?

A significant appreciation of a country's currency can affect businesses in several ways:

  • Exports become more expensive and potentially less competitive in international markets
  • Imported raw materials and goods become cheaper
  • Domestic tourism may decrease as it becomes more expensive for foreign visitors
  • Overseas investments and profits are worth less when converted back to the domestic currency
  • Businesses may face pressure to reduce prices to remain competitive with cheaper imports
  • Companies with significant overseas operations may see a decrease in the value of their foreign earnings
  • Potential for job losses in export-oriented industries
  • Opportunities for expansion or acquisition of foreign assets at relatively lower costs
Businesses may need to adapt their strategies, such as focusing more on domestic markets, hedging currency risks, or seeking cost efficiencies to remain competitive.

Flashcard: Globalisation

What are the main advantages and disadvantages of globalisation for businesses?

Advantages:

  • Access to new markets
  • Economies of scale
  • Access to global talent and resources
  • Increased opportunities for growth

Disadvantages:

  • Increased competition
  • Cultural and language barriers
  • Complexity of international operations
  • Exposure to global economic risks
